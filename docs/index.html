Release intelligence

Categorical risk scoring for software releases

Predictive release governance and incident intelligence methods, demonstrated end to end on fully public data. Five yes/no questions asked of every change, a threshold rule that turns the answers into protocol, and no severity scores anywhere.

The 5R framework

5R is a categorical risk classification applied to every release note before test planning. Each category is stamped yes or no, with quoted evidence from the note text. There are no weights: the output of scoring is a protocol, not a number.

R1Data Structure

The change touches schema, stored data shape, calculations, or interpretation logic.

R2Blast Radius

The change touches high-volume processes, batch jobs, or critical feeds.

R3Integration

The change affects downstream consumers, interfaces, APIs, or reports.

R4Hidden Complexity, the hard gate

The note hides behind innocuous labels (performance, refactor, cleanup, minor) or is three sentences or fewer. The note is held until its author and a technical lead jointly re-score it.

R5Rollback

The change involves one-way operations, forward-only migrations, or anything that cannot be cleanly reversed.

Threshold rule. Zero stamps proceeds normally. One stamp triggers that category's testing protocol. Two or more add a coordination sign-off before planning begins.

Shifting the questions left

At release-note time a stamp is a discovery: the change already exists and the gate can only react. Asked earlier, when functional specs are created from requirements, each stamp becomes an authoring obligation instead: R1 demands an explicit data contract in the spec, R3 an enumerated list of consumers, R5 reversibility designed in rather than bolted on. R4 becomes an intake gate on the requirement itself, which is where innocuous labels originate. Release-time scoring then verifies that what was built still matches the risk profile the spec declared. The full argument is in the shift-left note.

Workstreams

5R scoring on public release notes99 notes scored

GitLab's monthly release notes, versions 19.0 to 19.2, split into 99 individual items and scored note by note against the five categories, with quoted evidence per stamp. Integration risk (R3) is the most common surface at 41% of notes; 41% of notes carry two or more stamps and would need a coordination sign-off; 38% trip the R4 hard gate, almost always on brevity. The widest-impact change in the corpus, a mandatory PostgreSQL major-version upgrade, arrived as a two-sentence note: the brevity gate caught what a severity scale would have had to guess at. Method, scored data, and worked examples are in 5r-release-scoring.

CrowdStrike Falcon outage: a 5R retrospectiveretro scored

A retrospective 5R scoring of the CrowdStrike Falcon outage of 19 July 2024, worked entirely from CrowdStrike's own published post-incident review and root cause analysis. Scored from its own description, the change stamps all five categories, yet it travelled as "a content configuration update" released "as part of regular operations": the R4 hard gate on routine-label language is the centrepiece, and CrowdStrike's own remediation list (canary rings, staged deployment, contract validation, subscribable release notes) converges on the protocols a categorical gate triggers from the description alone. AI Incident Database: topics, tone, and a governance lens60 incidents scored

Topic and tone modelling across 1,571 public AI incident records, plus a 5R governance lens asking whether each incident's failure mode maps to a software-change risk category. Synthetic media and deception is the largest theme in the corpus, ahead of autonomous vehicles or facial recognition. On a 60-incident sample, the lens declines to map 47% of incidents to any 5R category at all, most are about human intent or negligence, not a system's own risk shape, and where it does map, data and interpretation failures (R1) dominate at 32% of the sample, with blast-radius failures (R2) appearing in none of it.
